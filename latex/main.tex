\begin{abstract}
The limited availability of annotated medical imaging data is a major challenge for deep-learning based object detection. Generative Adversarial Networks have been proposed as a potential solution by generating synthetic images for data augmentation and have shown promising results in medical image classification and segmentation. However, their effectiveness in object detection remains underexplored.

This work investigates the impact of CycleGAN-based data augmentation on pulmonary nodule detection in chest CT images. Experiments are conducted on a subset of the LUNA16 dataset using the YOLO11 object detection framework. Four training settings are compared under identical hyperparameters: a baseline setting trained on real data only, baseline training with appearance-level augmentations, training on a combination of real and CycleGAN-generated images and a combination of appearance-level and CycleGAN-based augmentation.

On a fixed validation set, CycleGAN increased AP@0.5:0.95 and Mean IoU relative to the baseline but marginally reduced AP@0.5; appearance-level, label-preserving augmentation improved all metrics, and their combination with CycleGAN-generated images achieved the best yet modest results. 

These findings indicate that appearance-level synthesis offers little benefit for object detection and complements, rather than replaces, classical augmentation. Given that classical augmentation improved all metrics at a friction of the computational cost, utilizing CycleGAN for solely appearance robustness is difficult to justify.

This study concludes that gains reported for GAN-based augmentation in classification and segmentation does not directly translate to object detection; future work should explore anatomy-aware, 3D or diffusion-based synthesis to better model geometric variability.
\keywords{Medical Object Detection, Generative Adversarial Networks, CycleGAN, YOLO, Data Augmentation}
\end{abstract}
